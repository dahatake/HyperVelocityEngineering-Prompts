## 2. Company Overview

**Basic Company Information:** (*This chapter provides an overview by treating loyalty programs themselves as a business domain, rather than focusing on a specific company.*) Since loyalty programs originated in the U.S. airline industry (frequent flyer programs) in the 1980s, they have **become widespread** across a wide range of industries, including retail, dining, hotels, automotive, and financial services. Particularly over the past decade (2015–2025), advances in digital technology have driven broad adoption from large enterprises to SMEs, and the market has grown at an annual rate exceeding 20%. For example, the loyalty management market reached approximately **$6 billion** in the mid-2020s and is projected to expand to about $28.6 billion by 2030. For many companies, operating a loyalty program is a core component of marketing strategy and is positioned as a type of business unit or **Customer Relationship Management (CRM) function** in its own right. [[trueloyal.com]](https://www.trueloyal.com/resources/loyalty-statistics/)

**Business Domains and Main Services:** Typical loyalty programs include the following formats/services:

* **Points-based Programs:** Points are awarded based on spending amount or frequency and can be exchanged for rewards/prizes once a certain number is reached (e.g., retail chain point cards, airline miles).
* **Status/Tiered Membership:** Members are assigned ranks (Bronze/Silver/Gold, etc.) based on purchase amount or frequency, and higher tiers receive better benefits (higher discounts, dedicated services, etc.) (e.g., elite status programs in hotels and airlines).
* **Subscription-based Memberships:** Paying a flat fee provides free shipping, discounts, and special services (e.g., Amazon Prime charges an annual fee and offers multiple value-adds such as delivery benefits and video streaming; as of 2025 it has over <span><strong>260 million</strong></span> members worldwide).
* **Stamp/Badge-based:** Collect stamps with each purchase and obtain prizes after a set number (ranging from paper stamp cards in restaurants to in-app badges).
* **Coalition/Joint Programs:** Multiple companies issue and redeem common points in an alliance format (e.g., common airline alliance miles; points from co-branded credit cards can be exchanged for other companies’ products).

**Main services** that support such programs include dedicated mobile apps, member web portals, customer support channels, and data analytics services. In recent years, **instant point balance checks and coupon delivery via mobile apps** have become standard, enabling users to use programs seamlessly with just a smartphone. Some leading companies have also started **integrating with chatbots and voice assistants**, making it increasingly possible to operate by voice, e.g., “Tell me my current point balance,” “Order products with points.”

**Mission/Vision:** A common vision among companies running loyalty programs is not to remain a mere promotional tool but to **“build long-term, mutually valuable relationships with customers.”** In other words, they aim for two-way value creation: “maximize customer lifetime value by enhancing customer loyalty while returning value to customers through rewards.” Many companies advocate “customer centricity” and “building trust with customers” as management philosophies, and loyalty programs serve as concrete initiatives to realize those aims. For example, <Company>Starbucks</Company> values a “personal relationship with each customer” and operates Starbucks Rewards integrated with its mobile app. There, by using customer data to provide individualized coupons and by fostering community (e.g., member-only events), the program functions as **part of the brand experience****.

## 3. As-Is Analysis (Current State)

### 3.1 External Environment Analysis (PEST / 5 Forces)

**(a) PEST Analysis:** The macro-environmental factors surrounding loyalty programs are organized using the PEST framework.

* **Political:** Strengthened consumer protection and personal data protection regulations in each country affect program operations. In particular, the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, effective 2018) places major restrictions on the use of personal data in loyalty programs. Companies are required to obtain **explicit consent** at program enrollment and ensure transparency of data usage purposes; opt-outs must be honored even in marketing via points. In some countries, regulations require loyalty points to be treated as **paid liabilities (deferred revenue)** for accounting purposes, making financial management important. Politically, antitrust considerations may also arise, with debates over competition restrictions by large programs.

* **Economic:** Macroeconomic trends also affect customer loyalty. During expansions, consumption increases and companies expand point awards, whereas during downturns they may **reduce costs** by revisiting benefit content—or conversely run point-doubling promotions to stimulate consumption. Over the past decade, the **2019–2020 COVID-19 pandemic** battered the travel and dining industries, causing many customers to lose opportunities to use existing loyalty benefits. As a result, temporary measures such as point expiration grace periods and membership status extensions were observed. In the post-COVID recovery phase, inflation has made customers more value-conscious, increasing expectations for **“value-for-money” loyalty benefits**. Meanwhile, customer acquisition cost (CAC) has risen about 50% over the past five years, increasing the economic rationale for companies to focus more on **retaining existing customers**. [[trueloyal.com]](https://www.trueloyal.com/resources/loyalty-statistics/)

* **Social:** Changes in consumer lifestyles and values also affect program design. With the rise of digital natives, preferences for “experiences tailored to oneself,” “immediacy,” and “gamification” are pronounced. For example, Millennials and Gen Z tend to prefer **personalized rewards** and experiential perks shareable on social media rather than uniform points. In increasingly busy lives, consumers dislike “cumbersome registrations and procedures,” so simple and seamless UX is required. In aging societies, program design that is easy for seniors (parallel use of physical cards and enhanced human support) becomes a social issue. Furthermore, perspectives on “customer loyalty” are shifting to emphasize **“value-based loyalty,”** where consumers resonate with **corporate missions they can empathize with** (e.g., contributions to sustainability) rather than seeking points alone. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)

* **Technological:** Technological progress has significantly evolved loyalty program formats. With the spread of smartphones, **mobile app platforms** have become mainstream, enabling location-based visit points and real-time coupon delivery via push notifications. Big data analytics and cloud computing have made it easier to run **segment-specific initiatives** by analyzing member data at the millions scale in real time. Particularly noteworthy in recent years is the introduction of **AI (Artificial Intelligence)**. Machine learning-based churn prediction models and recommendation engines are already being implemented by many companies. Moreover, with the advent of **Generative AI** and large language models (LLMs) gaining prominence since 2023, it has become possible to **automatically generate marketing copy** via prompts and deploy chatbots that respond to inquiries with human-like quality. For example, Hilton hotels have AI agents that automatically send personalized messages before and after stays, increasing direct booking rates by 15%. Another technological factor is experimental tokenization of points using blockchain (turning points into digital assets exchangeable across ecosystems). [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

From these PEST factors, the **environmental imperatives** are “advanced data use while complying with regulations,” “flexible responses to economic fluctuations,” “adapting to diverse customer needs,” and “differentiation through new technology.”

**(b) Porter’s 5 Forces (Competitive Forces) Analysis:** Next, we organize the competitive environment affecting the loyalty program business across the five forces.

* **Rivalry among Existing Competitors:** The **intensity of competition is extremely high**. In many industries, major companies are launching their own programs, and each company is vying with initiatives to “enhance customer loyalty.” Loyalty programs have become **“a taken-for-granted presence”** from the consumer perspective, and the basic value offered by each company (point accrual rates, discounts) tends to converge. As a result, differentiation is difficult, and there is a risk of falling into an excessive competition on point awards. Escalating benefits to avoid being outdone by competitors leads to rising costs and margin pressure; inferior programs directly drive customer churn. Thus, companies strive to enhance services while seeking a **balance with profitability**. **Alliances among competitors** (e.g., mutual point usage between airlines and hotels) are also frequent, giving rise to ecosystem turf wars. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)

* **Threat of New Entrants:** The technological hurdle of building a loyalty program has fallen with the spread of IT services, and even SMEs and startups can implement relatively easily using third-party platforms. In that sense, **entry barriers are not high**. There are cases of fintech firms building their own point economies and emerging retail companies rapidly growing with digitally native programs. However, running a program successfully at scale requires a **large customer base** and **ongoing financial investment**. It is not easy for new entrants to suddenly take away the millions to tens of millions of members and long-cultivated brand trust that incumbents possess. Overall, it is **easy to enter but difficult to scale**. More threatening than greenfield entrants can be entries from existing adjacent industries (e.g., tech companies launching point businesses in financial services).

* **Threat of Substitutes:** What substitutes loyalty programs themselves are those initiatives that achieve “customer loyalty enhancement” via different approaches. Examples include **subscription discounts (subscription models)**, **one-off deep discount campaigns**, or psychological loyalty through customer community-building. Models like paid memberships (e.g., Amazon or Uber offering immediate discounts/benefits rather than points) are a form of substitute (indeed, Uber ended its point-based “Uber Rewards” and consolidated into the subscription-based “Uber One”). While these substitutes offer **clear and immediate benefits** to users, companies must provide sufficient appeal to drive sign-ups. At present, there are limited moves to completely replace the points/miles mechanism itself; however, for **“point-fatigued” customers**, the need to combine alternative loyalty-building measures is growing.

* **Bargaining Power of Buyers:** Individual consumers are weak on their own but collectively exert very strong influence. This is because **customers can freely choose and use multiple programs**, so they can easily leave programs in which they see little value. In situations where an individual belongs to an average of 15 programs, it is easy to “switch” and use the most advantageous program for each purchase. Especially in markets with many similar products/services, buyers can use price comparison sites and reviews to find alternatives. Consequently, companies must **continuously present attractive offers** to customers, or active rates will quickly decline. In the modern era, dissatisfaction can spread via social media and affect brand image, so customer voices cannot be ignored. Conversely, if an excellent experience is provided, customers will actively repurchase. For example, one survey states “83% of loyalty program members say the program influences their decision to buy again with that brand,” indicating that winning customer support can create a powerful lock-in effect. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/) [[trueloyal.com]](https://www.trueloyal.com/resources/loyalty-statistics/)

* **Bargaining Power of Suppliers:** Here, “suppliers” refers to providers of services and goods necessary to run loyalty programs. Specifically, partners who supply point redemption goods, vendors of program operation systems (software companies), and providers of data analytics and AI tools. Companies that offer attractive products/services as point redemption options (e.g., popular event organizers) may possess strong bargaining power due to their exclusive value. IT platform companies likewise gain high pricing power if their loyalty management systems become market standards. On the other hand, companies often have multiple alternative procurement sources; for example, redemption goods can be provided from in-house inventory or via cross-deals with other companies. Overall, supplier bargaining power is **moderate**. However, in leading-edge technology (e.g., AI-equipped loyalty platforms) where the number of players is limited, suppliers (tech companies) tend to be in the driver’s seat—note this in particular.

From the 5 Forces analysis, the **cross-industry implication** is that “differentiation of loyalty programs and winning genuine customer loyalty” emerges as the greatest challenge. With intensifying competition and increased consumer choice, it is urgent for companies to revisit program value propositions and create uniqueness. Without pursuing benefits or experiences others do not offer, and efficient operations powered by advanced technology, the risk of becoming undistinguished increases.

### 3.2 Internal Environment Analysis (Resources & Capabilities)

Next, we analyze the internal resources and capabilities of companies operating loyalty programs. From a cross-industry perspective, we organize commonly observed internal factors and current states.

* **Organizational Structure & Talent:** In many companies, loyalty program operations are run by a specialized team under the marketing department or handled by a CRM department. Because they handle customer touchpoints across the organization, marketing, IT, customer support, and sometimes finance collaborate to manage programs. Increasingly important is placing **data analysts and data scientists**, who are expected to derive meaningful insights from customer behavior data. However, from a cross-industry perspective, there is a chronic shortage of such advanced talent. Especially SMEs may be unable to secure high-level analytics talent and thus fail to leverage accumulated data. Organizationally, **breaking down silos** to manage customer experience end-to-end is also a challenge. When customer data is managed separately by sales channels (online store, physical stores, etc.), integrated loyalty initiatives become difficult, making organization-wide governance important.

* **Technical Strength & IT Foundation:** Program operations are inseparable from IT. Large enterprises develop in-house or implement advanced packaged software (e.g., loyalty management modules from Salesforce or Oracle) to build systems that withstand scale at millions of members. Smaller organizations often rely on third-party cloud services (SaaS for loyalty point management). Current challenges include **legacy system constraints**. Older point management systems may be limited in real-time processing or flexible campaign setup and can hinder digital transformation. The lack of a **data integration foundation** is also common. Without a data lake that unifies online/offline customer behavior, fragmented data remains scattered by channel. Such technological delays create gaps that prevent maximizing the potential of **AI utilization** and **personalization**. Leading companies are investing heavily in IT foundations; for example, <Company>Starbucks</Company> uses a data platform and AI to analyze “hundreds of millions” of daily transactions in real time. As a result, they **classify customers into about 400,000 fine-grained segments** and automate optimal offer delivery, dramatically improving per-customer revenue. Thus, **differences in technical strength** directly translate into differences in program effectiveness. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

* **Talent & Capabilities (Marketing Strength):** Program success requires not only systems but also **compelling planning capabilities** and **meticulous operational excellence**. Marketing capabilities include designing campaigns based on customer insights, communication strategies (what to send, when, and via which channels), and negotiation skill for partnerships (planning tie-up benefits with external partners). Currently, many companies conduct routine point awards and coupon initiatives, but there are differences in proficiency regarding **fine-grained personalization** and **cross-channel integration strategies**. Managing customer engagement via multiple channels (email, SMS, app notifications) requires advanced omnichannel marketing capabilities; few companies (e.g., Hilton) optimize this with AI agents. Companies that can run data-driven and agile PDCA cycles quickly learn from experimental initiatives and improve programs. As capabilities are polarizing, **talent development and knowledge sharing** remain industry-wide challenges. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

* **Financials (Profitability & Efficiency):** Financially, **balancing costs and returns** is always under scrutiny. Major cost drivers include (1) benefits provisioning (point accrual reserves, prize costs), (2) system operating costs, and (3) personnel expenses. Points are generally booked as **deferred liabilities**, requiring future reserves, so estimating an appropriate breakage rate is critical for profit management. Efficiency indicators include “program operating cost/member” and “cost to acquire per member.” Currently, the cost per customer often ranges from several hundred to several thousand yen per year, but the key evaluation point is whether **returns via LTV uplift** exceed these costs. Leading cases report that by introducing AI to automate operations they **reduce operating costs by 25–50%** and simultaneously increase member purchase amounts by 15–25%. Overall, **properly run programs contribute to high profitability**, whereas insufficient programs risk increasing costs alone and squeezing profits, leading to variability in financial efficiency across companies. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l) [[trueloyal.com]](https://www.trueloyal.com/resources/loyalty-statistics/)

* **Operations & Supply Chain:** Operational aspects include procurement and delivery processes for benefits (rewards) and customer support. For retailers, this covers inventory management for point redemption products; for airlines, seat management for award tickets. The current trend is that benefit inventories are **not limited to the company’s own products/services**, and are increasingly diversified through partnerships (e.g., exchanging miles for hotel stays or e-money). From a supply chain perspective, contracts and coordination with partners are crucial in securing such external offerings. A trend in operational efficiency is the expansion of **digital self-service**. Programs are designed so members can complete point redemption, bookings, and inquiries directly via the app, reducing manual handling. This includes the use of chatbots and voice AI. For example, Delta Air Lines’ AI chatbot automatically processes 60% of inquiries from loyalty members and cuts response time from 24 hours to just 3 minutes. Thus, AI and automation are advancing in operations as well, seeking both efficiency and service improvements. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

In summary, the internal environment indicates a **gap between leading companies advancing in talent, organization, technology, and finance, and companies sticking with old methods**. In particular, whether **data integration and AI utilization** are in place has become a critical internal factor that significantly affects loyalty program performance.

### 3.3 Business Portfolio Analysis (BCG Matrix, etc.)

This section evaluates businesses and initiatives related to loyalty programs from a portfolio perspective. From a cross-industry viewpoint, we apply the BCG matrix hypothetically, assuming a typical company deploys multiple loyalty initiatives and analyzing each along the axes of “market growth” and “market share.”

* **“Stars (High Growth, High Share)”:** New-generation loyalty initiatives leveraging digital technology fall here. For example, **integrated loyalty platforms centered on mobile apps** and **AI-driven personalized coupon delivery** are rapidly growing and can create competitive advantages. Cases like Starbucks and Sephora (cosmetics) lead the market with advanced engagement strategies via their own apps. Continued focus of investment on these star businesses is essential to nurture them into future cash cows. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

* **“Question Marks (High Growth, Low Share)”:** Initiatives in emerging areas whose position within the company is not yet established. For example, **subscription-based paid memberships** (Amazon Prime-like models) are being explored by many companies, yet few dominate at present. Traditional retailers newly launching paid memberships fall into this category—growth potential is large but success models remain uncertain. **Blockchain-enabled point circulation markets**, innovative but requiring market education, also fit here. These require selective trial and concentration—escalate to stars if successful or consider withdrawal if not.

* **“Cash Cows (Low Growth, High Share)”:** Mature, existing loyalty initiatives. For instance, **traditional point card schemes** (plastic cards, paper stamp cards) have limited growth potential but maintain high share at certain companies due to long operational histories. Airline mileage programs are typical cash cows with memberships in the tens of millions and are in the mature stage yet remain mainstays of retention and revenue. Large growth investments are unnecessary here; the focus is on efficient operation and milk (securing profit). For example, selling miles (monetizing by selling miles to banks and partners) generates cash.

* **“Dogs (Low Growth, Low Share)”:** Initiatives with neither growth nor share. Examples include **outdated simple discount coupon distribution** and **partnership programs with weak appeal**. Programs with inferior competitiveness see little use and only incur costs; such “dogs” should be divested quickly to reallocate resources to promising areas.

Organizing loyalty-related initiatives from a portfolio perspective suggests focusing on **new initiatives leveraging digital & AI**, while **reviewing legacy initiatives** to optimize resource allocation. Management should allocate resources to the Star and Question Mark areas (mobile-centric advanced programs, paid memberships, new cross-company business models) to build future competitive advantages.

### 3.5 SWOT Analysis

Based on the external/internal environment and competitive landscape above, we organize the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats for the target company (i.e., a typical loyalty program operator).

**Strengths:**

* **Extensive Customer Data and Foundation:** Accumulating large volumes of purchase history and behavioral data through years of program operation is a major strength. This is an asset that others cannot easily replicate and can be leveraged for diverse marketing initiatives, depending on analysis. In fact, revenue contribution from existing customers is high (65% of sales come from repeat customers), and rich data is the core of customer understanding and cross-sell strategies. [[trueloyal.com]](https://www.trueloyal.com/resources/loyalty-statistics/)
* **Presence of Brand-loyal Customers:** There is a core fan segment that has long used the loyalty program and has a strong attachment to the brand. They have higher cumulative spend and higher-than-average LTV, and they can serve as a source of new customer referrals through word-of-mouth. One study states that “customers emotionally connected to a brand have a 306% higher lifetime value,” and having such loyal customers already is a strength. [[trueloyal.com]](https://www.trueloyal.com/resources/loyalty-statistics/)
* **Channel Network and Operational Strength:** Owning extensive customer touchpoints through store networks and online platforms is also a strength. Programs can provide seamless experiences such as instant point accrual and immediate coupon usage, and operational standardization/efficiency is advanced. Accumulated know-how for large-scale program operations (member inquiry handling, fraud monitoring, etc.) is also a strength that new entrants lack. For example, companies like <Company>American Airlines</Company> manage over a million members and have introduced fraud detection AI, enabling advanced operations. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

**Weaknesses:**

* **Obsolescence of Value Proposition:** Programs that have long relied on similar point awards/discounts have lost freshness and are less attractive to customers. Many programs **“fail to present a differentiated value proposition,”** which is a weakness. BCG research also shows that “many program members do not feel meaningful value for themselves,” and the decline in point value and lack of attractive benefits are partly responsible for low engagement. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)
* **Complex User Experience:** Complicated membership registration, unclear point calculations, and hurdles to benefit redemption (e.g., high required points) represent major UX weaknesses. Particularly, the “lack of simplicity” drives churn; complex rules and procedures push users away. This can be seen as a byproduct of incremental operations on existing systems; fundamental improvements are necessary. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)
* **Lag in Data Utilization and Technology Adoption:** Data exists internally but is underutilized; outdated systems prevent flexible trials of new marketing initiatives. Companies slow to adopt AI and real-time analytics have limited personalization and rely on **uniform mass email blasts**, which are less effective. As competitors begin leveraging advanced technologies, inadequate in-house tech capabilities and insufficient strategies for using external partners are weaknesses that are hard to overcome in the short term.
* **Rigid Cost Structure:** Some programs have fixed costs (point accrual rates, maintenance costs) making it difficult to change operations in response to changing conditions. Companies with large point balances (liabilities) fear waves of point redemptions triggered by program changes, hindering agile strategic shifts. Organizationally, operating “as a mere extension of the past,” and lacking fresh ideas, also constitutes rigidity.

**Opportunities:**

* **Leveraging Generative AI and Advanced Technologies:** As noted, AI progress is a major opportunity. Especially, generative AI enables **large-scale automation of personalized content creation**, significantly boosting marketing efficiency and effectiveness. Integrating conversational AI like ChatGPT can provide 24/7 concierge service to members. Very few companies can fully leverage this today; early investment creates differentiation. Opportunities also include blockchain-based point exchange markets and IoT-linked location-based benefit delivery. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l), [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)
* **External Partnerships:** Partnerships with other companies open up opportunities to leverage each other’s customer bases. Building **common loyalty platforms** that allow inter-industry point interoperability is particularly promising. For instance, creating a cross-vertical points alliance across retail, dining, and travel can encompass everyday life. In Japan, ecosystems like T-Point and Rakuten Points exist, but there is also a chance to lead such platforms. Collaboration with fintech (e.g., linking with personal finance apps to incorporate points into asset management) can create new value. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)
* **Developing Emerging Markets/Segments:** Geographically, expanding into emerging markets and, domestically, penetrating programs into underserved segments such as rural areas and seniors represent growth opportunities. In regions where smartphone adoption is still growing, introducing simple app-based programs can yield first-mover advantages. For seniors, programs with operational support or phone assistance can expand share. Moreover, for luxury brands where loyalty initiatives have historically been less prevalent, introducing loyalty without breaking brand ethos could become a new revenue source.
* **Shifts in Consumer Preferences:** Incorporating consumers’ growing environmental/social awareness into programs can create resonance. For example, mechanisms to “donate points to social contributions” or award bonus points for eco-friendly behavior enhance brand value. In fact, IBM research indicates “44% of consumers tend to choose brands aligned with their values,” and leveraging programs to showcase CSR aspects can be a differentiator. [[trueloyal.com]](https://www.trueloyal.com/resources/loyalty-statistics/)

**Threats:**

* **Market Saturation and Engagement Decline:** As previously indicated, loyalty programs are saturated, and **engagement is declining** due to consumer program fatigue/boredom. Surrounded by too many membership cards and apps, individual brand loyalty is diluted (“commoditization of loyalty”). If companies fail to keep customers interested, increasing membership counts will not translate into meaningful revenue contributions, undermining program legitimacy. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)
* **Tightening Regulations and Privacy Concerns:** Data privacy regulations are likely to become stricter in various countries, threatening **constraints on personal data usage**. Strengthened cookie regulations and amended privacy laws make detailed tracking and use of third-party data more difficult, potentially affecting personalization accuracy. In the worst case, consumers may avoid enrollment out of discomfort with data collection. There is also the risk of consumer protection regulations on point expiration and forfeiture rules.
* **Abrupt Changes in the Competitive Environment:** Entries from other industries and large tech companies can rapidly alter the competitive landscape. For example, if Apple or Google deploy large-scale services at the intersection of finance and loyalty, or if super-apps like WeChat or Alipay spread globally, traditional loyalty schemes may be overshadowed. The market may tip toward winner-takes-all, risking **non-use of a company’s points**. In the future, if AI agents optimally choose purchases on behalf of consumers (e.g., automatically deciding “where it’s most advantageous to buy”), point appeals may become powerless unless incorporated into AI algorithms. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)
* **Impacts of Economic Deterioration:** Global economic downturns or a resurgence of unexpected pandemics could chill consumer sentiment. In such times, “non-essential” benefits and premium member services are often cut first, potentially reducing program budgets. On the consumer side, frugality may lead to not using points at all and hoarding them—or conversely, mass redemption (increasing the company’s burden). Both scenarios stress financial and operational systems.

In summary, the SWOT indicates a strategy to **leverage internal strengths (data/customer base) and external opportunities (AI/partnerships) while addressing internal weaknesses (insufficient value/technology lag) and external threats (saturation/regulation)**. How to bridge these gaps is the focus of the To-Be strategy in the following chapters.

## 4. To-Be Vision (Target State)

Based on the issues revealed in the current-state analysis, we envision the medium- to long-term **“To-Be”** for loyalty program strategy. This chapter discusses the future vision and strategic direction, the prioritization of associated challenges, and new growth opportunities.

### 4.1 Vision and Strategic Direction

**Medium- to Long-term Vision:** The target company should evolve from merely providing a points service to a **“comprehensive engagement platform that elevates customer experience value.”** Concretely, the vision includes:

* **Optimized Experiences for Each Customer:** Provide experiences tailored to each customer’s needs/preferences at every touchpoint—from product proposals to benefit delivery and communications. Aim for a state where customers receive the proposals they want at the times they want, as if they had a **personal concierge** (the pinnacle of hyper-personalization).
* **Fostering Emotional Loyalty:** Beyond material rewards, cultivate psychological connections and empathy with the brand. Aim for a loyalty program where customers feel **joy and pride** in belonging to the brand’s community. Build bonds beyond transactional relationships through member-only events, spaces to share UGC (user-generated content), and opportunities to participate in social contribution activities.
* **Virtuous Cycle of Revenue Growth and Customer Value:** Establish a virtuous cycle in which the program significantly increases LTV (customer lifetime value), and the incremental revenue is reinvested to further improve customer experiences. By doing so, maintain competitive advantage in the medium to long term and be recognized as the No. 1 loyalty program in key markets (“When people think of that brand, they think of that program.”).

**Target Market Position:** To achieve the above vision, aim to attain the following market positions:

* **Top-tier Loyalty Metrics in the Industry:** Achieve levels significantly above the industry average for customer retention, annual member spend, and NPS (Net Promoter Score). For example, **No. 1 repeat rate** and No. 1 customer recommendation intent in core target markets.
* **A Brand Synonymous with “Customer-Centricity”:** Build a reputation not only for excellent products/services but also for “outstanding commitment to customer loyalty.” This creates positive effects for new customer acquisition and becomes a long-term brand asset.
* **Cross-Industry Leadership:** Become an innovation leader not just within your industry but across loyalty marketing. Pursue initiatives so advanced that companies in other industries say, “We want to model our program on yours.” If possible, consider productizing loyalty strategy itself into a service by offering your program platform and consulting to other companies, opening a new revenue stream.

We organize the **strategic challenges** to be solved to realize the vision and prioritize them based on impact and urgency. The most important medium- to long-term challenges that emerged from the current-state analysis are as follows:

1. **Advanced Personalization:** *(Top priority and high impact)*
   Correct the issue of uniform offers sent to many customers and enable appropriately customized information/benefits at every touchpoint. This is the **most critical challenge** to dramatically increase customer engagement. Improving personalization accuracy directly boosts revenue (via higher offer response rates), so this warrants top-priority investment. 2. **Innovation in Value Proposition (Rewards):** *(Top priority)*
   Revisit the program’s value proposition itself and create appeal that customers can get “only with this program.” Shift from mere discounts/point accrual to **value-added, experiential, and emotional benefits** (e.g., fan events, access to exclusive content). Overcome the current weakness of stale benefits and differentiate from competitors; this is a top-priority challenge. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/), [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)

3. **Data Integration and AI Infrastructure:** *(High priority)*
   Build the data/AI infrastructure foundation for personalization and advanced analytics. Consolidate siloed customer data into an integrated platform that enables real-time analysis and scoring. Also build platforms for machine learning models and generative AI, along with AI talent and governance frameworks. This will **greatly improve insight extraction and execution speed**, becoming a source of long-term competitiveness. While impact is extremely large, buildout takes time, so prioritize as a medium-term effort. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

4. **UX Simplification and Omnichannel Integration:** *(Medium priority)*
   Redesign UX so customers feel programs are “easy to use and easy to understand.” Specifically, simplify member registration (one-click sign-up), revisit point accrual/redemption processes (e.g., auto-accrual, instant discounts), and integrate channels (online, stores, call centers) for consistent experiences. While UX improvements are essential for satisfaction and active rates, they must proceed in parallel with tech foundation buildout; thus medium priority.

5. **Partnership Strategy:** *(Medium priority)*
   Consider an ecosystem strategy such as cross-industry partnerships and participating in common point networks. Seek **strategic partners** that complement value you cannot offer alone and provide comprehensive benefits to customers. Options include strengthening co-branded credit cards, integrating with other apps, and common IDs. 6. **Organizational Capability Building:** *(Medium priority)*
   Internal structures/skills are indispensable to execute all of the above. This includes hiring/developing data science talent, establishing a cross-functional loyalty taskforce, and fostering a culture of KPI monitoring. Organizational transformation takes time to yield results, so start early and continue steadily.

Among these, **1. Advanced Personalization** and **2. Value Proposition Innovation** are the most urgent and critical themes. These are keys to preventing churn and boosting engagement in the short term; pursue them early in parallel with other initiatives. As a foundation, plan investments in **3. Data & AI infrastructure**, and do not miss medium-term growth opportunities through **4. UX improvements** and **5. partnership expansion**. **6. Organizational capability building** underpins the execution of all.

### 4.3 Identification of Growth Opportunities

Within the To-Be strategy, we present several concrete **new growth opportunities**:

* **(I) Expansion into New Markets/Segments:**
  Broaden the base of loyalty programs by focusing beyond current core customer segments. Consider, for example, creating a sub-brand program for younger users with stronger gamification, offering programs that can be completed via phone or paper at stores for seniors, or expanding common points in emerging markets with local partners. For Gen Z, who are highly digital, provide SNS-linked and game-like rewards (e.g., collecting digital badges) to boost new member acquisition. Also consider strengthening B2B loyalty, such as point systems for distributors/resellers, which is often untapped and can be a new growth driver.

* **(II) Added Value via New Products/Services:**
  Upgrading the loyalty program itself with new services is also a growth opportunity. Introducing a **paid premium membership** is one such approach. In addition to the free standard program, provide higher-tier benefits via monthly/annual fees (free shipping, dedicated customer support, early access, etc.). This becomes a stable revenue source and further enhances loyalty. Also consider a **white-label loyalty platform business**: externalize your system to support other brands’ loyalty construction (operated as a multi-tenant managed service) to generate new business income. Some major airlines already consult externally with their mileage systems and know-how; templating your expertise could be a business opportunity.

* **(III) Business Model Transformation (Digitalization & Globalization):**
  Redefine the business model via digital technologies and global expansion. **Tokenization of points** is one example—circulating value on blockchain to turn loyalty points into a form of digital currency tradable on secondary markets, building a new economy. This could raise point value and enable more precise incentive design of user behavior. Also consider participation/leadership in **cross-industry loyalty federations**. An integrated program that covers everyday life (shopping, travel, entertainment with one set of points) is highly convenient; if led well, your company can build a favorable ecosystem. Partnerships with global enterprises and common rules are required, making this a long-term vision; if realized, first-mover advantages are massive. Establishing a **highly autonomous AI-driven operating model** is also a transformation: in the future, AI agents could automatically optimize marketing per customer, enabling operations to run with minimal human intervention and shifting from variable cost-centric models to scalable fixed-cost-centric models. This would facilitate consistent high-quality programs across global locations and ease international expansion. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

These To-Be growth opportunities involve **innovation** beyond extensions of existing business. Capturing them requires strategic thinking unbound by convention and a degree of risk-taking; if successful, the **potential business value** over the medium to long term is very large. For example, monetizing a paid membership with several million subscribers can change the revenue structure, and owning a common platform as a market standard can lead the competition through network effects. Therefore, in addition to incremental improvements from the As-Is state, position these growth plans within the roadmap.

## 6. Strategic Recommendations

To realize the transformation from As-Is to To-Be, we propose the following **strategic action plan**. We organize initiatives to be executed in stages over short, medium, and long terms, discuss their rationales and expected effects, and address KPIs and monitoring to manage progress.

### Recommended Strategies and Rationales

**(1) Build a Customer Data Platform and Advance AI Utilization**
*– Core strategy: Strengthening the foundation for personalization/efficiency*

The top priority is to construct a **Customer Data Platform (CDP)** that integrates scattered internal customer data and enables real-time analysis, and to introduce machine learning and generative AI in stages. Specific recommended actions:

* **Data Integration and Cleansing:** Consolidate customer profiles, transaction histories, web/app behavior logs, and customer support histories to create a single customer view. This improves accuracy of understanding “who follows which behavior patterns,” enhancing segmentation and LTV estimation.
* **Real-time Processing Foundation:** Build a cloud-based data lake; use streaming to enable immediate point accrual after purchase and instant offer delivery upon behavior detection. This returns **real-time feedback** to customers and maintains engagement.
* **AI Algorithm Introduction:** Initially implement machine learning models for churn prediction, purchase prediction, and behavior clustering. In the medium term, use generative AI/LLMs for automated content creation and advanced chatbots. For example, “AI generates the next email body → a human reviews and sends,” balancing efficiency and effectiveness. Leading companies have increased response rates severalfold with personal offers. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

**Rationale:** This resolves the weakness of underutilized data and maximizes the value of customer data assets (a strength). Aramis’s research reports outcomes such as “15–30% engagement uplift, 20–40% improvement in personalization accuracy, and 25–50% reduction in operating costs” through AI, indicating strong ROI. Other cases (e.g., Starbucks) also show that building a robust data & AI foundation is the wellspring of competitive advantage. Thus, this strategy is the **game changer** that underpins all initiatives in loyalty strategy. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

**(2) Reconstruct Loyalty Value Proposition (Reward Reform)**
*– Differentiation strategy: Renew the program into something compelling and unique*

Next, overhaul the core “value proposition” of the program. Escape competition on accrual rates and expand **high value-add, high-engagement** benefits. Recommended actions:

* **Introduce High-affinity Rewards:** Develop benefits customers value beyond monetary conversion. Examples: “Invite members above a certain annual spend to new product launch events”; give top-tier members the title of brand ambassador and partial participation in product development, providing **experiential** and **status/community value**—thereby strengthening psychological loyalty.
* **Expand Benefits via Partnerships:** Use partnerships to offer attractive benefits not available alone. Build a network of benefits across categories such as reciprocal point exchange with airlines/hotels and special deals at fine restaurants/theme parks. Let existing points be usable for everyday services (e.g., movie tickets, ride-hailing) to **cover daily life** and enhance lock-in—while controlling costs through partnerships. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)
* **Launch a Paid Premium Membership:** In addition to current free membership, establish a paid premium tier with higher benefits (e.g., free shipping, priority support, exclusive coupons—an Amazon Prime-like model). Provide **tiers of loyalty** so enthusiastic customers willingly pay while others remain engaged in the free tier, expanding revenue opportunities.

**Rationale:** This addresses the Weakness of “insufficient benefit appeal” and creates a value proposition competitors cannot easily match. Loyalty & Reward Co notes that “lack of value proposition” is a primary cause of program failure; consulting cases show dramatic improvements in perceived member value through benefits enhancement. Unique, appealing rewards become a durable strength and deepen loyalty. Paid memberships directly contribute to revenue and motivate free members to “graduate” to paid tiers. As with Amazon Prime, more subscribers drive lock-in and cross-sell, forming a virtuous cycle. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/) [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/), [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)

**(3) Optimize Engagement Communications**
*– Touchpoint strategy: Advance timing, channels, and messaging*

Optimize the quality and frequency of customer communications (campaign notifications, follow-ups, contact handling) to **consistently engage customers in appropriate ways**. Recommended actions:

* **Omnichannel Integration and AI Agents:** Integrate management of multiple touchpoints (email, SMS, app notifications, direct mail, in-person) and deliver consistent messaging. Further, have AI agents adjust communication by the optimal channel/time/frequency for each customer. For instance, push notifications for lapsed customers as reminders and detailed emails for active customers—i.e., personalized touchpoint strategies. Hilton’s case shows AI-selected timing across the customer journey **raised engagement by 22%**. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)
* **Community-building and Two-way Dialogue:** Move beyond one-way marketing and nurture communities for interaction among customers and with the company. Operate member-only SNS groups/forums and hold regular roundtables/feedback events with high-value members, showing a stance of **reflecting customer voice in management**—deepening engagement as customers feel part of the program’s operation.
* **AI-enabled Contact Centers:** Introduce AI chatbots/voice systems for member inquiries and issue resolution to accelerate and enhance responses. Delta handles 60% of inquiries via AI and cut response times to 3 minutes. Similar adoption can improve satisfaction and efficiency. Feed accumulated inquiry content back into service improvement. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

**Rationale:** Raising engagement requires continual communication that delivers “the right content at the right time in the right way.” Current one-size-fits-all blasts miss opportunities. Combining AI with an omnichannel strategy provides each customer with the best touchpoint, dramatically improving loyalty. Recent outcomes at Hilton and Delta support this. Community-building reduces churn and increases LTV; insights from social listening and two-way dialogue inform service improvements and new benefit development, creating a virtuous cycle. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l), [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

**(4) UX Redesign and Simplicity of Loyalty**
*– Foundational strategy: Fundamental improvement of user experience*

Revisit the program’s overall user experience to make it **intuitive for anyone to use and continue using**. Recommended actions:

* **Improve Member Onboarding:** Minimize friction in new member registration. Online: social login and one-click sign-up leveraging existing customer info. Offline: provisional enrollment via a simple SMS at purchase. Wendy’s increased registration completion **by 23%** with AI-driven personalized onboarding; improving onboarding efficiency expands the member base. [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)
* **Simplify Usage:** Redesign daily operations—checking balances, using coupons, redeeming rewards—for simplicity. In the mobile app, display core actions on the home screen so a single tap shows current tier, points, and recommended benefits. In non-digital channels, standardize operations such as using a phone number at checkout to apply points. Since complexity deters use, make **“keep it simple”** the UX mantra. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)
* **User Testing and Continuous Improvement:** Conduct usability tests inside and outside the company and regularly evaluate ease-of-use from the member’s perspective. Use feedback to rapidly run PDCA cycles, refining UX. Interview non-engaged segments (those inactive after registration) to identify and remove obstacles. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)

**Rationale:** UX complexity is one of today’s weaknesses, and resolving it directly increases both membership and usage frequency. Loyalty & Reward Co notes that “non-simple programs fail to convey value and see lower engagement”; conversely, making the program simple and understandable encourages active participation. Although this may appear less directly revenue-driving than AI adoption, it is a **foundational strengthening**. Easier programs accumulate more usage data, which in turn improves AI accuracy—creating synergy. [[loyaltyrewardco.com]](https://loyaltyrewardco.com/break-through-loyalty-program-saturation/)

**(5) Strategic Partnerships and Ecosystem Expansion**
*– Expansion strategy: Gaining network effects through partnerships*

Expand program value and reach through collaboration with other companies and industries rather than operating alone. Recommended actions:

* **Point Interoperability:** Proactively participate in existing common point networks (e.g., converting JAL miles to Ponta points) or build new frameworks led by your company. In the future, aim to increase your presence in a **“point economy”** where points flow seamlessly across industries. Customers gain greater utility from your points, which raises loyalty; your company gains opportunities to access other companies’ customers. ### KPIs and Monitoring

To measure the effects of strategy execution and adjust course, set **KPIs (Key Performance Indicators)** and conduct thorough monitoring. Main examples and target images:

* **Customer Metrics:** Number of members (especially active), customer retention rate (annual continued usage), revenue per user (RPU), NPS, engagement rate (response rate to offers). For example, retention +5ppt, NPS top tier, etc.
* **Financial Metrics:** Annual sales growth of members, share of program-related revenue, LTV, program operating cost ratio, ROI. For example, member sales +15%, differential vs. non-members +○○%, ROI 150%+, etc. ROI is especially important for investment prioritization; compute by initiative.
* **Operational Efficiency:** Campaign lead time (days from planning to execution), AI proposal adoption rate, average inquiry handling time. For example, halve handling time vs. current (Delta achieved −95%). [[aramisllc.com]](https://aramisllc.com/our-blog/i8ngwurfcuk77bb8fd81mgan4ity5l)

**Monitoring Framework:** Review these KPIs monthly/quarterly by management and responsible departments. Build dashboards for real-time visibility and alerts for anomalies. For AI-dependent areas, conduct regular human evaluations (e.g., monitor whether “AI recommendation precision” stays above thresholds) and adjust models if issues arise, ensuring **governance**. Establish forums to aggregate frontline customer voices from marketing/sales/CS, and tune initiatives based on qualitative insights not visible in metrics.
